The problem

Mass streaming services
Own the digital
Artists with millions of streams earn less than minimum wage. Tracks under 1,000 plays earn zero. The middle class of music is being deliberately de-monetized to fund the top 1%.
Live Nation · Ticketmaster
Own the physical
A federal jury found Live Nation guilty of illegal monopoly on April 16, 2026. They control 86% of major venues. Artists and fans are squeezed from both ends simultaneously.
The gap nobody has closed
You already built the audience. You just never got to own the relationship.
The connection that happens in a live room — when a crowd turns, when people stop talking and start singing back — disappears the second the night ends. Technology captured the music but lost the moment. Movement gives that moment somewhere to live.

The platform

The Stage
Live. Raw. Yours.
Live sessions, writing rooms, acoustic sets, behind-the-scenes access. Artists go live on their terms. Fans get proximity to the creative process. Every live moment is a subscription event worth paying for.
The Vault
Your archive. Your terms.
Every moment on Stage feeds the Vault automatically. Demos, recordings, setlists, unreleased work. Artists control what's free, what's subscriber-only, what's locked. The catalog belongs to the artist. Always.
Discovery + The Collective
The scene is back.
Fans discover artists the way they always did — through other artists. Established artists co-sign emerging ones. When a Juno Award-winning artist champions a band nobody's heard of, that band gets access to an audience that already knows how to listen. The scene expands the way scenes always have — one true believer at a time.
The share moment
It's not the viral. It is the virus.
When something happens on Stage — a song lands, an artist says something real — fans hit one button. Not share to Movement. Share to everywhere. A clip, a moment, a direct link back to that artist's discovery page. The fan becomes the ambassador. Every share is a portal. Every portal is a new fan finding their front door.
Concert listings
The digital leads back to the physical.
The Stage builds the relationship. The show is where you consummate it. Movement connects the digital moment to the live event. A fan who has been subscribed for six months and finally shows up to a show is not a casual attendee. They are a true believer. In a world where Ticketmaster just lost in federal court, artists listing and selling directly through Movement is both a statement and a structural advantage.

Who gets value

Stakeholder
The established artist
Direct revenue. Fan relationships they actually own. Status as a scene-maker through the Collective. A platform that works in their interest — not against it.
Stakeholder
The emerging artist
A path forward that doesn't require algorithmic luck. Co-signs that mean something. Access to established audiences. A place to build from the first ten fans.
Stakeholder
The fan
Proximity to artists they love. Discovery through trust, not algorithms. The feeling of "I found this band" — and a frictionless way to prove it to everyone they know.

The Collective Fund
"It's not charity. It's not philanthropy. It's the digital version of what Jimi Hendrix did when he pulled an unknown band onto his stage. Except now it has a financial mechanism underneath it."
When an established artist hits a revenue milestone on Movement, a percentage flows into a Collective Fund they direct toward artists they believe in. The established artist gains status and scene influence. The emerging artist gets visibility, resources, and a co-sign that means something. The platform gets a self-sustaining ecosystem where success breeds more success instead of concentrating at the top. This is what "powered by all of us" actually means.

The moment

"Mass streaming services own the digital. Ticketmaster owns the physical. Both are now visibly, publicly, provably corrupt. Movement is the alternative to both of them simultaneously — and it arrives the week a federal jury handed us the opening line."
The ethos that cannot be copied
Every platform extracts from artists. Movement is structurally built to do the opposite. The 80/20 split is not a feature — it is a founding principle. The Collective is not a marketing initiative — it is a governance mechanism. The share moment is not a growth hack — it is the scene operating the way it always did, finally given a digital layer. This is not a better streaming service. This is the correction to the streaming service. And the music industry has never needed a correction more than it does right now.
